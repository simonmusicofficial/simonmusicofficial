thinkers whose work i admire

Reggie Watts TED Talk

In Reggie Watts’ TED Talk, titled “Reggie Watts disorients you in the most entertaining way,” he does exactly that, bringing silly humor and/in music to enliven a normally straightforward platform that is TED. Reggie Watts is one of my biggest artistic, musical and philosophical inspirations, in my entire life. In this TED Talk, he moves between almost incoherent declarations that exist between languages, but vaguely language we can recognize, sometimes mixing in actual phrases from those proposed languages. He also breaks up his talk/performance into musical breaks, where he creates loop-based improvisational songs that range from soul, to gibberish, hip-hop, spiritual vocal runs and groovy funk. Even his presence- it’s exciting, in a very unique way. His big afro, abstract and colorful t-shirt, hiking pants and shoes… he walks around the stage and is completely captivating and energetic. It is inspiring to me and relevant in how I approach my music. I am inspired by how free he seems - where is this gibberish coming from? Did he spend enough time listening to these languages or talking to people or listening to people to internalize enough of the basic syllables and pronunciations to be able to replicate an accurate, recognizable “impression” of a language? That makes people laugh? And then moving directly into wonderfully groovy looped improvisations. I find it incredible. I want to be as free as he is in my music. He is a philosophical genius, evoking understanding and connection without meaning.

Elise Roy TED Talk

While listening to Elise Roy, I was reflecting on my experience with debilitating mental health issues, and how I believe I could have been supported better in recovering and finding jobs that specifically support and understand that experience and at least have a respect for it. I have felt misunderstood at so many food service jobs the past few years, especially being neuro-divergent and non-binary, with a history of severe mental issues, I feel like I have a great capacity to be a really welcoming presence. I am not physically disabled, but I relate to the struggle of debilitating conditions that make it more difficult to function in society. This relates to my project because of how I also see that being neuro-divergent for me lends itself to being more creative and having unique, sometimes weird, sometimes fascinating ideas and musical/artistic creations. Coupled with radical anti-fascist views, this brings me to the fact that my art can be very provocative, and I must be aware and sensitive to that. In the healing process I am currently in, I see that that will take years, not just moments. So, in considering a 5-year Odyssey plan, it is helpful to allow myself the space and time to experience a non-linear progression of healing, but also of continuing to cultivate my creativity and artistic expression.

Carol Dweck TED Talk

In Carol Dweck’s TED Talk, titled “The Power of Believing You Can Improve,” she discusses topics such as “not yet” ideology, praising the process, “the tyranny of now,” fixed vs. growth mindset, and most importantly for me, to “dream big dreams.” This talk is meaningful to me in how she both reminds me of the essential learning curve in life and in specific pursuits - that life and progress are non-linear, so again it’s important to “praise the process.” I am a process and practice-based artist, so this is resonant in being focused day to day on slowly, patiently building a craft in music, which for me has manifested the past few years in starting every song with drums, no matter what the song becomes. Another aspect of Dweck’s talk that resonated with me was the idea of “dreaming big dreams,” and honoring that instinct and personal visualization that can be so valuable, I’ve found, for self-confidence, healthy ego, and profound experiences on my path towards (hopefully) success. There is also an importance with fighting back against the “tyranny of now” in this sense of big dreams, in having the perspective that success takes time, and that it takes a growth mindset vs. a fixed mindset to really build something sustainable in life, whether that be work, passion, or love, for example.

emergent strategy by adrienne marie brown

The last piece of work that has been truly inspiring, especially this spring, is adrienne marie brown’s book, “emergent strategy,” and specifically, the included discussion of the concept of the “fractal.” brown describes the fractal as the “relationship between small and large,” a never ending pattern, whose ultimate effect is that “How we are at the small scale is how we are at the large scale.” How I take this is that similar to the “butterfly effect,” in that even the most minute action is inevitably, infinitely interconnected with every other particle and action in the universe, and that that small action is thus reflected also on the large scale, and looks massive to an infinitely smaller moment/action/particle. She talks of the Fibonacci sequence as well, and then moves towards a sentence mirrored in Naropa University’s vision (where I currently attend), “Transform yourself to transform the world.” This is the end result, but in terms of what resonates with me from the discussion of the fractal is that my day-to-day life, days added up over time, create a beautiful (almost) infinite mosaic and impact on the world, even though one individual day might feel insignificant. In terms of musical process, this manifests in how I continue to stay focused, again day-to-day, with continuity in a musical/creative process that I believe in. Tangibly, this again looks like always starting songs with drums, no matter what the song becomes. This is mirrored by brown’s discussion of the fractal in how over time, I create a repertoire of dance music that has a common thread - similar to the thread of humanity that connects all of us.

personal note from the artist

my current 5-year plan is my musical path. In the first year, (that I am currently in), my goal is to perform live as a DJ as much as possible. Last year I performed live twice, so my goal is to do much more than that and start playing regularly at clubs in Boston near where I live, so that I can both get my music out and start making a living off of music. With this experience, I want to move to New York City, perform more, and then move to Berlin to join the techno scene there. Second year will be focused on recording my third album, moving to Utah, doing promotion for my music, and performing regularly in a scene. The third year will be a tour year, in the US and Europe. The fourth year will be focused on securing a record deal, based out of somewhere in the US such as Boston, NYC or California, or best case scenario in Berlin. The fifth year will be my ultimate goal, which is playing at large music festivals around the world.
